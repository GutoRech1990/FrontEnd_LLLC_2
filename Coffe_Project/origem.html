The Fascinating Origin of Coffee

Coffee has a rich and fascinating history that intertwines with cultures and traditions over centuries. It is believed that the discovery of coffee occurred around the 9th century in the region that is now Ethiopia, but the true origin of the plant and its use as a stimulating beverage remains shrouded in legend.

According to the most famous legend, a goat herder named Kaldi observed that his animals behaved strangely after eating red berries from an unknown shrub. The goats were full of energy, jumping and running even after a full day of grazing. Intrigued, Kaldi tried the berries himself and felt an increase in energy and vitality. The story goes that he brought the berries to a local monk, who initially disapproved of the beans and threw them into the fire. To his surprise, the heat released an irresistible aroma. The roasted beans were then taken from the embers and dissolved in hot water, creating the first cup of coffee.

Despite its legendary nature, this story reflects what was likely a gradual process of discovery, as people in the mountainous regions of Ethiopia and Yemen began to use coffee fruits to make a drink that gave them energy.

The Journey of Coffee Through the Middle East

From the Ethiopian highlands, the cultivation and use of coffee spread to the Arabian Peninsula around the 15th century. Historical records indicate that the Sufis, a mystical Islamic order in Yemen, were the first to regularly consume coffee. They used the drink during long nights of meditation and prayer, taking advantage of its stimulating properties to keep their minds alert and focused.

In the Yemeni city of Mocha, coffee began to be cultivated on a large scale, and the city soon became an important center for coffee trade. The port of Mocha sent coffee beans to various parts of the Middle East, North Africa, and later, Europe. This era gave rise to the famous term “mocha coffee,” in homage to the port city.

In Mecca, coffee quickly became popular, with the first coffeehouses, known as "qahveh khaneh," appearing in the 16th century. These places soon became gathering centers where people of all social classes met to chat, listen to music, play chess, and discuss current events. Coffeehouses were hubs of intellectual and cultural activity, which led some rulers to fear they could become centers of rebellion and dissent. At times, attempts were made to ban coffee, but they failed as the love for coffee continued to grow.

Coffee Reaches Europe

Through trade routes with the Middle East, Venetian merchants brought coffee to Europe in the 17th century. Initially, the drink was met with suspicion. Some referred to it as “Arabian wine,” and it was even condemned by religious figures, who saw it as a threat to Christianity. However, after Pope Clement VIII tasted coffee and blessed it, the drink's popularity spread quickly across Europe.

Coffeehouses began to appear in major cities such as Venice, London, Paris, and Vienna, becoming social and intellectual hubs. In England, these coffeehouses were called “Penny Universities” because, for a penny, people could buy a cup of coffee and gain access to intellectual conversations and discussions on politics, philosophy, and science. Some of these coffeehouses, like Café Procope in Paris and Lloyd’s Coffee House in London, became so influential that they led to the founding of institutions such as insurance companies and newspapers.

The Global Expansion and Rise of Coffee Plantations

In the early 18th century, the demand for coffee in Europe was so great that colonial nations sought to expand the cultivation of the plant to their tropical colonies. The Dutch were the first to introduce coffee in their Southeast Asian colonies, such as Java, now part of Indonesia, and the French took the plant to the Caribbean, specifically to Martinique.

In South America, coffee found an ideal environment for its cultivation. Brazil, which received its first coffee plants in the early 18th century, quickly became the world's largest producer, a position it still holds today. The vast coffee plantations in Brazil, as well as in other Latin American countries like Colombia, Costa Rica, and Guatemala, became the primary source of supply for the global coffee market.

Coffee in the Modern World

Today, coffee is one of the world's most valuable commodities, and millions of people depend on it, both as consumers and as workers in production and distribution chains. It remains a beverage that transcends borders and cultures, taking on different forms and rituals around the world.

Each country and culture has a unique way of preparing and enjoying coffee. In Italy, espresso is king, while in Turkey, coffee is prepared with finely ground coffee and boiled water in a small pot called cezve. In Nordic countries, coffee consumption per capita is among the highest in the world, with a preference for strong, pure filtered coffee.

Coffee has also given rise to countless variations, such as cappuccino, latte, mocha coffee, and many other combinations that are part of the daily routine of millions of people. Additionally, with the rise of the third wave of coffee movement, coffee appreciation has turned into a true art, focusing on alternative brewing methods, artisanal roasting, and the valorization of coffee bean origins.

Whatever way you prefer to enjoy your coffee, it certainly carries with it a rich history and a tradition that continues to reinvent itself, making coffee not just a drink but a true way of life.
